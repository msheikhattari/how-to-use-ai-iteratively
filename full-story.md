# The Worked Example — A 4-Hour Conversation That Compressed 18 Months

A real example. One person. One conversation with Claude. Started at "24-28 months conservative estimate" — ended at "10-14 months realistic" for the same goal. Same AI, same person — the difference was iteration method.

This is what the method looks like in practice.

## The setup

A working professional wanted to map a path to a complex multi-year credential — a Paramedic certification combined with eventual humanitarian-medicine work. He'd been thinking about it loosely for months. He opened a conversation with Claude.

Standard expectation: 2-3 years of full-time effort + significant savings + leaving current career.

## What actually happened

The conversation went through 7 distinct phases over about 4 hours. Each phase started with him NOT accepting the previous answer.

### Phase 1: Initial framing

He asked about the credential path. AI gave the standard options — typical providers, typical durations, typical sequence.

**AI's initial estimate:** 24-30 months to full credential. 1,200-1,800 program hours. Multiple gated requirements.

### Phase 2: Verification

He linked a specific state-licensing source he'd found. The actual state requirement was **1,100 hours**, not 1,200-1,800. The AI's number was a national-typical default, not state-specific.

He also pushed: "Where does this requirement about prior field experience come from?" The AI checked — and discovered that the "required prior experience" was actually NOT required for the program; the program provided the field experience internally. The AI had inserted a 6-12 month buffer that wasn't necessary.

**Timeline after verification:** 18-24 months, not 24-30. ~6 months compressed by verification alone.

### Phase 3: Compression hypothesis

He asked: "Can I do this faster?"

AI surfaced accelerated programs — full-time intensives in other states, hybrid online programs, accelerated boot camps. Compared them.

**Several options surfaced** that the AI hadn't volunteered initially. None of them got more than 6 weeks compression on their own.

### Phase 4: Deep research

He asked: "Do deep research on whether ANY compression method exists. Self-study, transfer credit, lower-clinical-hour states — investigate all of it."

The AI spawned 4 parallel research agents investigating different angles.

**Findings:**
- 3 of the "fast" programs he'd been told about were actually misframed (marketing claims that didn't hold up — one "11-month" program was actually 16-21 months)
- 1 specific program (PERCOM Online) genuinely allowed self-paced asynchronous didactic
- An overlooked state regulation: the requirement could be satisfied by "12 months of experience OR 150 documented field calls"

That alternative requirement could be completed in 4-5 months of intensive work, not 12 months elapsed.

**Timeline after deep research:** 14-15 months from today.

### Phase 5: Self-pacing + parallel tracks

He pushed further: "Within an accelerated program, can I work harder during specific weeks to compress further? I'd be willing to do 40-hour weeks of program work during specific blocks."

The AI explored. Found that PERCOM specifically allowed truly self-paced didactic. Found that clinical hours could be densely scheduled (back-to-back shifts during research-time blocks). Found that multiple program tracks could run in parallel.

**Identified the binding constraint:** monthly skills-lab cadence at one location set a 7-month floor for that portion.

**Timeline after self-pacing analysis:** 12-14 months realistic.

### Phase 6: Capacity unlock

He volunteered context he hadn't mentioned: "I have about half the year as 'research time' at my job — flexible time blocks for personal projects."

This changed everything. The wave-based pattern (intensive during research time, sustained during normal weeks) became viable. The AI recalculated.

**Timeline after capacity unlock:** 10-14 months realistic.

### Phase 7: Final plan + verification queue

He asked: "Build me a wave-based execution plan with concrete checkpoints. And a list of questions I need to verify with the institutions involved."

AI delivered:
- Phase-by-phase plan with checkable items per phase
- Skill tree visualization
- 14-item verification queue (calls he needs to make)
- Wave-pattern tempo design with burnout prevention
- Cost estimate

Total: ~10-14 months realistic. Potentially 10-11 months if institutional negotiation succeeds on one specific binding constraint.

## What he did differently each phase

| Phase | His move | What it unlocked |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | Verified specifics against primary source | Removed 6-month buffer that wasn't real |
| 3 | Pushed for compression | Surfaced options AI hadn't volunteered |
| 4 | Demanded deep research, not surface options | Found alternative requirement that 95% of advisors miss |
| 5 | Pushed for within-program compression | Identified true binding constraint vs flexible scheduling |
| 6 | Volunteered capacity context | Changed framing from "standard professional" to "wave-based" |
| 7 | Demanded executable plan, not options | Got checklist + skill tree, not menu |

## The cost of NOT pushing back

If he'd accepted the first answer:
- 24-28 month timeline expectation
- Significantly higher cost (would have chosen wrong programs)
- Wrong sequencing (might have wasted months on prerequisites that weren't actually required)
- Probably would have given up — 2-3 years feels too long for a side commitment

By pushing back through 6 phases:
- Realistic 10-14 month timeline
- Right program identified (PERCOM)
- Right sequencing (parallel tracks + wave-based tempo)
- Renewed commitment (10-14 months feels achievable for a side commitment)

The compression wasn't AI insight. It was the AI following the human's iterative pushback. Each phase started with him NOT accepting the previous answer.

## What this means for your problem

You probably have a hard problem you've thought about for months. Maybe years.

The AI's first answer to it is probably median. Conservative buffer included. Standard sequencing assumed. Generic constraints presented.

**Try this:**

1. Open Claude.
2. Ask your question.
3. Whatever the first answer is, don't accept it.
4. Use the 7 patterns from [the-method.md](./the-method.md).
5. Iterate for 30+ minutes.

See what shifts.

You might find your "this would take years" problem actually compresses to months. Or your "this is too expensive" problem actually has financing structures you didn't know about. Or your "this requires X credential" problem has alternative pathways you weren't told about.

The method works. The first answer is the starting point, not the destination.

## One last note

The conversation that produced this case study was ~4 hours. Most people won't spend 4 hours iterating with AI in one sitting. That's fine.

You can do this in 30-minute chunks across a week. Or 2-hour chunks across a month. The method works whenever you choose to apply it.

The key isn't the duration. It's the stance: **AI is a thinking partner, not an oracle. Your job is to engage with it like one.**
