# Prompts That Work

Concrete prompts you can copy and adapt. Organized by what you're trying to unlock.

## When you get a number that feels off

> "Walk me through the reasoning for that number. What's the floor vs the conservative default? Cite a primary source if possible."

> "Is that the typical estimate or the actual floor? What would have to be true for the floor to be lower?"

> "I found a source saying [different number]. Reconcile."

## When you want to compress a timeline

> "What in this plan can run in parallel that I might be doing sequentially?"

> "Walk through each step. For each, mark whether it must come before the next step, or can run alongside."

> "Where are the dependencies? Where are the false dependencies that look like dependencies but aren't?"

## When you want to verify specifics

> "Cite a primary source for [specific claim]. URL if available. If you don't have one, say so."

> "What would I need to call/check to verify this is current and accurate?"

> "List the institutions or providers you're recommending. For each: name, URL, current status, last verified."

## When you hit a constraint

> "Is this requirement actually fixed, or is there an alternative form of it that's more compressible?"

> "Who would I call to find out if this constraint has any flexibility?"

> "What would have to be true for [the apparent constraint] to be negotiable?"

## When AI doesn't know your specific situation

> "Here's my actual capacity: [X hours/week during regular periods, Y hours/week during intensive periods, available between dates Z]. Re-plan with this context."

> "I have these existing skills: [list]. Don't give me beginner advice; assume I know these."

> "I have these existing constraints: [list]. Don't assume standard 40-hour-work-week pattern."

## When something seems impossible

> "What would have to be true for this to be possible? Who would I call to find out?"

> "This is the answer if I accept the standard institutional framing. What's the answer if I'm willing to push, negotiate, or take a non-standard path?"

> "Has anyone done this in less time / for less money / with less infrastructure? Tell me about the outliers."

## When AI gives you a menu of options

> "You gave me 5 options. Pick one. Which would you actually do, and why?"

> "Tell me what you'd do if it were your career/business/decision. Be specific."

> "Convert this menu into a phase-by-phase plan with concrete actions per phase."

## When you need executable next steps

> "Give me a checklist of the next 7-10 actions, in order, with specific institutions/contacts/URLs for each."

> "What's the absolute next concrete action I could take today/this week?"

> "Build me a skill tree showing how each step unlocks the next. What's gated by what?"

## When you want to verify the plan is good

> "What are the failure modes of this plan? Where am I most likely to get stuck?"

> "Who would push back on this plan, and what would they say?"

> "What's the strongest critique of this approach? Steelman the opposite path."

## When you've been working on a plan and want a sanity check

> "Read back the plan we've built. What's missing? What's weak? What's overoptimistic?"

> "If you saw this plan on a resume or proposal, what would your first reaction be?"

> "Compare this plan to what 90% of people in my situation would do. How does it differ?"

## When you're stuck on a specific deliverable

> "I'm trying to [specific deliverable — write the cold email, design the page, scope the project]. Here's context: [...]. Don't give me 5 options; give me one draft I can edit."

> "Here's my attempt: [paste]. What's weakest about it? What would you change?"

## A meta-prompt for working with AI

At the start of any complex planning conversation:

> "I'm going to push back on your first answers because I know they're usually conservative defaults. Don't take it personally. When I push, give me sharper specifics or admit uncertainty. Don't give me option menus — give me your actual recommendation. Verify sources where you can. Tell me when you don't know."

## Custom prompts for your domain

The prompts above are generic. The real power comes from prompts tailored to your specific work.

**For a consultant pricing their work:**

> "I have these specific credentials: [list]. Most consulting AI advice is for generic consultants. What's the top-of-band pricing for someone with this specific credential mix?"

**For a builder pricing their work:**

> "I'm 16 and have these skills + paying client experience. Don't give me median teen-builder advice. What's the most aggressive pricing realistic given that profile?"

**For someone planning a credential path:**

> "I want to compress this credential path as much as legitimately possible. I'm not trying to skip requirements; I'm trying to find the fastest realistic execution. Walk through every requirement and tell me what's compressible vs binding."

**For someone scoping a client engagement:**

> "Here's the client's stated need: [...]. What questions should I ask before quoting? What scope creep is most likely on this kind of project?"

## The principle behind the prompts

Each of these prompts is doing one of two things:
1. **Pushing the AI to give a sharper answer** (verify, parallelize, reframe constraint, commit to recommendation)
2. **Giving the AI context it didn't ask for** (your capacity, your skills, your constraints)

Both moves push past the median. Use whichever fits the moment.

## Practice

Don't try to memorize these. Internalize the underlying moves:

- "Walk through the reasoning"
- "What can run in parallel?"
- "Cite a source"
- "Is this constraint actually fixed?"
- "Here's my capacity..."
- "Who could I call?"
- "Build the plan, not the menu"
- "Failure modes?"
- "Steelman the opposite"
- "Give me one draft, not five options"

Pick the prompt that fits the moment. After a few weeks, you'll be doing this without thinking.
